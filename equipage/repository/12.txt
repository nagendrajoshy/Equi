<p>Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it.<sup id="cite_ref-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: left;">—<a href="/wiki/George_Santayana" title="George Santayana">George Santayana</a></div>

<p><b>History</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="el" xml:lang="el"><i>ἱστορία</i></span> - <i>historia</i>, meaning "inquiry, knowledge acquired by investigation"<sup id="cite_ref-JosephJanda_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JosephJanda-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup>) is the discovery, collection, organization, and presentation of information about past events. History can also mean the period of time after writing was invented. Scholars who write about history are called <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historians</a>. It is a field of research which uses a <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">narrative</a> to examine and analyse the sequence of events, and it sometimes attempts to investigate objectively the patterns of cause and effect that determine events.<sup id="cite_ref-evans1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans1-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Historians debate the nature of history and its usefulness. This includes discussing the study of the discipline as an end in itself and as a way of providing "perspective" on the problems of the present.<sup id="cite_ref-evans1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans1-2"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tosh1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tosh1-4"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The stories common to a particular culture, but not supported by external sources (such as the legends surrounding <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a>) are usually classified as <a href="/wiki/Cultural_heritage" title="Cultural heritage">cultural heritage</a> rather than the "disinterested investigation" needed by the discipline of history.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Low1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Low1-8"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Events of the past prior to written record are considered <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a>.</p>
<p>Amongst scholars, the fifth-century BC Greek historian <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> is considered to be the "father of history", and, along with his contemporary <a href="/wiki/Thucydides" title="Thucydides">Thucydides</a>, forms the foundations for the modern study of history. Their influence, along with other historical traditions in other parts of their world, have spawned many different interpretations of the nature of history which has evolved over the centuries and are continuing to change. The modern study of history has many different fields including those that focus on certain regions and those which focus on certain topical or thematical elements of historical investigation. Often history is taught as part of primary and secondary education, and the academic study of history is a major discipline in University studies.</p>

<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:History-Dielman-Highsmith.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/History-Dielman-Highsmith.jpeg/220px-History-Dielman-Highsmith.jpeg" width="220" height="102" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:History-Dielman-Highsmith.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.19/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<i>History</i> by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Dielman" title="Frederick Dielman">Frederick Dielman</a> (1896)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A derivation from <i>*weid-</i> "know" or "see" is attested as "the reconstructed etymon <i>wid-tor</i> ["one who knows"] (compare to English <i>wit</i>) a suffixed zero-grade form of the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_root" title="Proto-Indo-European root">PIE</a> root <i>*weid-</i>-'see' and so is related to Greek <i>eidénai</i>, to know "...<sup id="cite_ref-JosephJanda_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JosephJanda-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-etym_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etym-9"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Polytonic" title="Polytonic" class="mw-redirect">ἱστορία</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> (<i>hístōr</i>) means "inquiry","knowledge from inquiry", or "judge". It was in that sense that <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> used the word in his <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a href="/wiki/History_of_Animals" title="History of Animals">Περὶ Τὰ Ζῷα Ἱστορίαι</a></span><sup id="cite_ref-Ferrater-Mora_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferrater-Mora-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> (<i>Perì Tà Zôa Ηistoríai</i> "Inquiries about Animals"). The ancestor word <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἵστωρ</span> is attested early on in <a href="/wiki/Homeric_Hymns" title="Homeric Hymns">Homeric Hymns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heraclitus" title="Heraclitus">Heraclitus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athenian</a> <a href="/wiki/Ephebos" title="Ephebos">ephebes</a>' oath, and in <a href="/wiki/Boeotia" title="Boeotia">Boiotic</a> inscriptions (in a legal sense, either "judge" or "witness", or similar).</p>
<p>The word entered the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> in 1390 with the meaning of "relation of incidents, story". In <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>, the meaning was "story" in general. The restriction to the meaning "record of past events" arises in the late 15th century. It was still in the Greek sense that <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a> used the term in the late 16th century, when he wrote about "<a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">Natural History</a>". For him, <i>historia</i> was "the knowledge of objects determined by space and time", that sort of knowledge provided by <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a> (while <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> was provided by <a href="/wiki/Reason" title="Reason">reason</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">poetry</a> was provided by <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a>).</p>
<p>In an expression of the linguistic <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">synthetic vs. analytic/isolating dichotomy</a>, English like Chinese (史 vs. 诌) now designates separate words for human history and <a href="/wiki/Storytelling" title="Storytelling">storytelling</a> in general. In modern German, French, and most Germanic and Romance languages, which are solidly synthetic and highly inflected, the same word is still used to mean both "history" and "story".</p>
<p>The adjective <i>historical</i> is attested from 1661, and <i>historic</i> from 1669.<sup id="cite_ref-Whitney_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whitney-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Historian</i> in the sense of a "researcher of history" is attested from 1531. In all <a href="/wiki/European_languages" title="European languages" class="mw-redirect">European languages</a>, the substantive "history" is still used to mean both "what happened with men", and "the scholarly study of the happened", the latter sense sometimes distinguished with a capital letter, "History", or the word <i><a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiography</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferrater-Mora_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferrater-Mora-11"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Historians%27_History_of_the_World_-_Title_Page.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/The_Historians%27_History_of_the_World_-_Title_Page.jpg/220px-The_Historians%27_History_of_the_World_-_Title_Page.jpg" width="220" height="323" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Historians%27_History_of_the_World_-_Title_Page.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.19/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The title page to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Historians%27_History_of_the_World" title="The Historians' History of the World">The Historians' History of the World</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Historians write in the context of their own time, and with due regard to the current dominant ideas of how to interpret the past, and sometimes write to provide lessons for their own society. In the words of <a href="/wiki/Benedetto_Croce" title="Benedetto Croce">Benedetto Croce</a>, "All history is contemporary history". History is facilitated by the formation of a 'true discourse of past' through the production of narrative and analysis of past events relating to the human race.<sup id="cite_ref-Whitney_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whitney-12"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The modern discipline of history is dedicated to the institutional production of this discourse.</p>
<p>All events that are remembered and preserved in some authentic form constitute the historical record.<sup id="cite_ref-wordnet_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wordnet-13"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The task of historical discourse is to identify the sources which can most usefully contribute to the production of accurate accounts of past. Therefore, the constitution of the historian's archive is a result of circumscribing a more general archive by invalidating the usage of certain texts and documents (by falsifying their claims to represent the 'true past').</p>
<p>The study of history has sometimes been classified as part of the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a> and at other times as part of the <a href="/wiki/Social_sciences" title="Social sciences" class="mw-redirect">social sciences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> It can also be seen as a bridge between those two broad areas, incorporating methodologies from both. Some individual historians strongly support one or the other classification.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In the 20th century, French <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a> <a href="/wiki/Fernand_Braudel" title="Fernand Braudel">Fernand Braudel</a> revolutionized the study of history, by using such outside disciplines as <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> in the study of global history.</p>
<p>Traditionally, historians have recorded events of the past, either in writing or by passing on an <a href="/wiki/Oral_tradition" title="Oral tradition">oral tradition</a>, and have attempted to answer historical questions through the study of written documents and oral accounts. For the beginning, historians have also used such sources as monuments, inscriptions, and pictures. In general, the sources of historical knowledge can be separated into three categories: what is written, what is said, and what is physically preserved, and historians often consult all three.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> But writing is the marker that separates history from what comes before.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">Archaeology</a> is a discipline that is especially helpful in dealing with buried sites and objects, which, once unearthed, contribute to the study of history. But archaeology rarely stands alone. It uses narrative sources to complement its discoveries. However, archaeology is constituted by a range of methodologies and approaches which are independent from history; that is to say, archaeology does not "fill the gaps" within textual sources. Indeed, Historical Archaeology is a specific branch of archaeology, often contrasting its conclusions against those of contemporary textual sources. For example, Mark Leone, the excavator and interpreter of historical <a href="/wiki/Annapolis,_Maryland" title="Annapolis, Maryland">Annapolis, Maryland</a>, USA has sought to understand the contradiction between textual documents and the material record, demonstrating the possession of slaves and the inequalities of wealth apparent via the study of the total historical environment, despite the ideology of "liberty" inherent in written documents at this time.</p>
<p>There are varieties of ways in which history can be organized, including chronologically, <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culturally</a>, territorially, and thematically. These divisions are not mutually exclusive, and significant overlaps are often present, as in "The International Women's Movement in an Age of Transition, 1830–1975." It is possible for historians to concern themselves with both the very specific and the very general, although the modern trend has been toward specialization. The area called <a href="/wiki/Big_History" title="Big History">Big History</a> resists this specialization, and searches for universal patterns or trends. History has often been studied with some practical or <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theoretical</a> aim, but also may be studied out of simple intellectual curiosity.<sup id="cite_ref-graham-ch1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-graham-ch1-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="rellink">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Protohistory" title="Protohistory">Protohistory</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_world" title="History of the world">history of the world</a> is the memory of the past <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experience</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens sapiens" class="mw-redirect">Homo sapiens sapiens</a></i> around the world, as that experience has been preserved, largely in written records. By "prehistory", historians mean the recovery of knowledge of the past in an area where no written records exist, or where the writing of a culture is not understood. <a href="/wiki/Human_history" title="Human history" class="mw-redirect">Human history</a> is marked both by a gradual <a href="/wiki/List_of_discoveries" title="List of discoveries">accretion of discoveries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_historic_inventions" title="Timeline of historic inventions">inventions</a>, as well as by <a href="/wiki/Quantum_leap" title="Quantum leap" class="mw-redirect">quantum leaps</a>—<a href="/wiki/Paradigm_shift" title="Paradigm shift">paradigm shifts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">revolutions</a>—that comprise epochs in the material and spiritual evolution of humankind. By studying painting, drawings, carvings, and other artifacts, some information can be recovered even in the absence of a written record. Since the 20th century, the study of prehistory is considered essential to avoid history's implicit exclusion of certain civilizations, such as those of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian" class="mw-redirect">pre-Columbian</a> America. Historians in the West have been criticized for focusing disproportionately on the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1961, British historian <a href="/wiki/E._H._Carr" title="E. H. Carr">E. H. Carr</a> wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<div class="Bug6200">The line of demarcation between prehistoric and historical times is crossed when people cease to live only in the present, and become consciously interested both in their past and in their future. History begins with the handing down of tradition; and tradition means the carrying of the habits and lessons of the past into the future. Records of the past begin to be kept for the benefit of future generations.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</blockquote>
<p>This definition includes within the scope of history the strong interests of peoples, such as <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginals" title="Australian Aboriginals" class="mw-redirect">Australian Aboriginals</a> and New Zealand <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a> in the past, and the oral records maintained and transmitted to succeeding generations, even before their contact with European civilization.</p>
<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Historiography">Historiography</span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">Historiography</a></div>
<p>Historiography has a number of related meanings. Firstly, it can refer to how history has been produced: the story of the development of <a href="/wiki/Historical_method" title="Historical method">methodology</a> and practices (for example, the move from short-term biographical narrative towards long-term thematic analysis). Secondly, it can refer to what has been produced: a specific body of historical writing (for example, "medieval historiography during the 1960s" means "Works of medieval history written during the 1960s"). Thirdly, it may refer to why history is produced: the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_history" title="Philosophy of history">Philosophy of history</a>. As a <a href="/wiki/Meta-level" title="Meta-level" class="mw-redirect">meta-level</a> analysis of descriptions of the past, this third conception can relate to the first two in that the analysis usually focuses on the narratives, <a href="/wiki/Language_interpretation" title="Language interpretation">interpretations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worldview" title="Worldview" class="mw-redirect">worldview</a>, use of <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a>, or method of presentation of other <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historians</a>. Professional historians also debate the question of whether history can be taught as a single coherent narrative or a series of competing narratives.</p>
<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy_of_history">Philosophy of history</span></h2>
<table class="toccolours" style="float:right; margin:0 0 1em 1em; width:250px; text-align:left; clear:right;">
<tr>
<td>
<div style="background:#f8eaba; text-align:center;">History's philosophical questions</div>
<ul>
<li>What is the proper unit for the study of the human past—the individual? The polis? The civilization? The culture? Or the nation state?</li>
<li>Are there broad patterns and progress? Are there cycles? Is human history random and devoid of any meaning?</li>
</ul>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_history" title="Philosophy of history">Philosophy of history</a></div>
<p>Philosophy of history is a branch of philosophy concerning the eventual significance, if any, of human history. Furthermore, it speculates as to a possible teleological end to its development—that is, it asks if there is a design, purpose, directive principle, or finality in the processes of human history. Philosophy of history should not be confused with historiography, which is the study of history as an academic discipline, and thus concerns its methods and practices, and its development as a discipline over time. Nor should philosophy of history be confused with the <a href="/wiki/History_of_philosophy" title="History of philosophy">history of philosophy</a>, which is the study of the development of philosophical ideas through time.</p>
<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_methods">Historical methods</span></h2>
<div class="rellink">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Historical_method" title="Historical method">Historical method</a></div>
<table class="toccolours" style="float:right; margin:0 0 1em 1em; width:250px; text-align:left; clear:right;">
<tr>
<td>
<div class="center">
<div class="thumb tnone">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ancientlibraryalex.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Ancientlibraryalex.jpg/220px-Ancientlibraryalex.jpg" width="220" height="223" class="thumbimage" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ancientlibraryalex.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/skins-1.19/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
A depiction of the ancient <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Alexandria" title="Library of Alexandria">Library of Alexandria</a></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<div style="background:#f8eaba; text-align:center;">Historical method basics</div>
<p>The following questions are used by historians in modern work.</p>
<ol>
<li><i>When was</i> the source, written or unwritten, produced (<a href="/wiki/Calendar_date" title="Calendar date">date</a>)?</li>
<li>Where was it produced (<a href="/wiki/Internationalization_and_localization" title="Internationalization and localization">localization</a>)?</li>
<li>By whom was it produced (<a href="/wiki/Authorship" title="Authorship" class="mw-redirect">authorship</a>)?</li>
<li>From what pre-existing material was it produced (<a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/analysis" class="extiw" title="wikt:analysis">analysis</a>)?</li>
<li>In what original form was it produced (<a href="/wiki/Integrity" title="Integrity">integrity</a>)?</li>
<li>What is the evidential value of its contents (<a href="/wiki/Credibility" title="Credibility">credibility</a>)?</li>
</ol>
<p>The first four are known as <a href="/wiki/Higher_criticism" title="Higher criticism" class="mw-redirect">higher criticism</a>; the fifth, <a href="/wiki/Lower_criticism" title="Lower criticism" class="mw-redirect">lower criticism</a>; and, together, external criticism. <i>The sixth and final inquiry</i> about a source is called internal criticism.</p>
</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>The historical method comprises the techniques and guidelines by which <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historians</a> use <a href="/wiki/Primary_source" title="Primary source">primary sources</a> and other evidence to research and then to <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">write history</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Halicarnassus" title="Halicarnassus">Halicarnassus</a> (484 BC – ca.425 BC)<sup id="cite_ref-lamberg-karlovsky-p5_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lamberg-karlovsky-p5-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> has generally been acclaimed as the "father of history". However, his contemporary <a href="/wiki/Thucydides" title="Thucydides">Thucydides</a> (ca. 460 BC – ca. 400 BC) is credited with having first approached history with a well-developed historical method in his work the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Peloponnesian_War" title="History of the Peloponnesian War">History of the Peloponnesian War</a>. Thucydides, unlike Herodotus, regarded history as being the product of the choices and actions of human beings, and looked at cause and effect, rather than as the result of divine intervention.<sup id="cite_ref-lamberg-karlovsky-p5_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lamberg-karlovsky-p5-20"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> In his historical method, Thucydides emphasized chronology, a neutral point of view, and that the human world was the result of the actions of human beings. Greek historians also viewed history as <a href="/wiki/Historic_recurrence" title="Historic recurrence">cyclical</a>, with events regularly recurring.<sup id="cite_ref-lamberg-karlovsky-p6_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lamberg-karlovsky-p6-21"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There were historical traditions and sophisticated use of historical method in ancient and medieval <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. The groundwork for professional historiography in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> was established by the <a href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a> court historian known as <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a> (145–90 BC), author of the <i><a href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji" class="mw-redirect">Shiji</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Grand_Historian" title="Records of the Grand Historian">Records of the Grand Historian</a>). For the quality of his timeless written work, Sima Qian is posthumously known as the Father of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Historiography" title="Chinese Historiography" class="mw-redirect">Chinese Historiography</a>. Chinese historians of subsequent dynastic periods in China used his <i>Shiji</i> as the official format for <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Four_Histories" title="Twenty-Four Histories">historical texts</a>, as well as for biographical literature.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources from April 2011">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Saint Augustine</a> was influential in <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Christian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_thought" title="Western thought">Western thought</a> at the beginning of the medieval period. Through the Medieval and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> periods, history was often studied through a <a href="/wiki/Sacred_history" title="Sacred history">sacred</a> or religious perspective. Around 1800, German philosopher and historian <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel</a> brought <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> and a more <a href="/wiki/Secular" title="Secular" class="mw-redirect">secular</a> approach in historical study.<sup id="cite_ref-graham-ch1_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-graham-ch1-17"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the preface to his book, the <i><a href="/wiki/Muqaddimah" title="Muqaddimah">Muqaddimah</a></i> (1377), the <a href="/wiki/Historiography_of_early_Islam" title="Historiography of early Islam">Arab historian</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sociology_in_medieval_Islam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sociology in medieval Islam (page does not exist)">early sociologist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a>, warned of seven mistakes that he thought that historians regularly committed. In this criticism, he approached the past as strange and in need of interpretation. The originality of Ibn Khaldun was to claim that the cultural difference of another age must govern the evaluation of relevant historical material, to distinguish the principles according to which it might be possible to attempt the evaluation, and lastly, to feel the need for experience, in addition to rational principles, in order to assess a culture of the past. Ibn Khaldun often criticized "idle <a href="/wiki/Superstition" title="Superstition">superstition</a> and uncritical acceptance of historical data." As a result, he introduced a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> to the study of history, and he often referred to it as his "new science".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> His historical method also laid the groundwork for the observation of the role of <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Systematic_bias" title="Systematic bias" class="mw-redirect">systematic bias</a> in history,<sup id="cite_ref-Mowlana_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mowlana-23"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> and he is thus considered to be the "father of historiography"<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Enan_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Enan-25"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> or the "father of the philosophy of history".<sup id="cite_ref-Akhtar_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Akhtar-26"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the West historians developed modern methods of historiography in the 17th and 18th centuries, especially in France and Germany. The 19th-century historian with greatest influence on methods was <a href="/wiki/Leopold_von_Ranke" title="Leopold von Ranke">Leopold von Ranke</a> in Germany.</p>
<p>In the 20th century, academic historians focused less on epic nationalistic narratives, which often tended to glorify the nation or individuals, to more objective and complex analyses of social and intellectual forces. A major trend of historical methodology in the 20th century was a tendency to treat history more as a <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a> rather than as an <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a>, which traditionally had been the case. Some of the leading advocates of history as a social science were a diverse collection of scholars which included <a href="/wiki/Fernand_Braudel" title="Fernand Braudel">Fernand Braudel</a>, <a href="/wiki/E._H._Carr" title="E. H. Carr">E. H. Carr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Fischer" title="Fritz Fischer">Fritz Fischer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Le_Roy_Ladurie" title="Emmanuel Le Roy Ladurie">Emmanuel Le Roy Ladurie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hans-Ulrich_Wehler" title="Hans-Ulrich Wehler">Hans-Ulrich Wehler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Trigger" title="Bruce Trigger">Bruce Trigger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marc_Bloch" title="Marc Bloch">Marc Bloch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Dietrich_Bracher" title="Karl Dietrich Bracher">Karl Dietrich Bracher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gay" title="Peter Gay">Peter Gay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fogel" title="Robert Fogel">Robert Fogel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucien_Febvre" title="Lucien Febvre">Lucien Febvre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Stone" title="Lawrence Stone">Lawrence Stone</a>. Many of the advocates of history as a social science were or are noted for their multi-disciplinary approach. Braudel combined history with geography, Bracher history with political science, Fogel history with economics, Gay history with psychology, Trigger history with archaeology while Wehler, Bloch, Fischer, Stone, Febvre and Le Roy Ladurie have in varying and differing ways amalgamated history with sociology, geography, anthropology, and economics. More recently, the field of <a href="/wiki/Digital_history" title="Digital history">digital history</a> has begun to address ways of using computer technology to pose new questions to historical data and generate digital scholarship.</p>
<p>In opposition to the claims of history as a social science, historians such as <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Trevor-Roper" title="Hugh Trevor-Roper">Hugh Trevor-Roper</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Lukacs" title="John Lukacs">John Lukacs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donald_Creighton" title="Donald Creighton">Donald Creighton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gertrude_Himmelfarb" title="Gertrude Himmelfarb">Gertrude Himmelfarb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Ritter" title="Gerhard Ritter">Gerhard Ritter</a> argued that the key to the historians' work was the power of the <a href="/wiki/Imagination" title="Imagination">imagination</a>, and hence contended that history should be understood as an art. French historians associated with the <a href="/wiki/Annales_School" title="Annales School">Annales School</a> introduced quantitative history, using raw data to track the lives of typical individuals, and were prominent in the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_history" title="Cultural history">cultural history</a> (cf. <i><a href="/wiki/Histoire_des_mentalit%C3%A9s" title="Histoire des mentalités" class="mw-redirect">histoire des mentalités</a></i>). Intellectual historians such as <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Butterfield" title="Herbert Butterfield">Herbert Butterfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Nolte" title="Ernst Nolte">Ernst Nolte</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Mosse" title="George Mosse">George Mosse</a> have argued for the significance of ideas in history. American historians, motivated by the civil rights era, focused on formerly overlooked ethnic, racial, and socio-economic groups. Another genre of <a href="/wiki/Social_history" title="Social history">social history</a> to emerge in the post-WWII era was <i><a href="/wiki/Alltagsgeschichte" title="Alltagsgeschichte">Alltagsgeschichte</a></i> (History of Everyday Life). Scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Martin_Broszat" title="Martin Broszat">Martin Broszat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ian_Kershaw" title="Ian Kershaw">Ian Kershaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Detlev_Peukert" title="Detlev Peukert">Detlev Peukert</a> sought to examine what everyday life was like for ordinary people in 20th-century Germany, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi" class="mw-redirect">Nazi</a> period.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marxist_historiography" title="Marxist historiography">Marxist historians</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Eric_Hobsbawm" title="Eric Hobsbawm">Eric Hobsbawm</a>, <a href="/wiki/E._P._Thompson" title="E. P. Thompson">E. P. Thompson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Hilton" title="Rodney Hilton">Rodney Hilton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georges_Lefebvre" title="Georges Lefebvre">Georges Lefebvre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eugene_D._Genovese" title="Eugene D. Genovese">Eugene D. Genovese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Deutscher" title="Isaac Deutscher">Isaac Deutscher</a>, <a href="/wiki/C._L._R._James" title="C. L. R. James">C. L. R. James</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Mason" title="Timothy Mason">Timothy Mason</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Aptheker" title="Herbert Aptheker">Herbert Aptheker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arno_J._Mayer" title="Arno J. Mayer">Arno J. Mayer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hill_(historian)" title="Christopher Hill (historian)">Christopher Hill</a> have sought to validate <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a>'s theories by analyzing history from a Marxist perspective. In response to the Marxist interpretation of history, historians such as <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Furet" title="François Furet">François Furet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Pipes" title="Richard Pipes">Richard Pipes</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._C._D._Clark" title="J. C. D. Clark">J. C. D. Clark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roland_Mousnier" title="Roland Mousnier">Roland Mousnier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ashby_Turner" title="Henry Ashby Turner">Henry Ashby Turner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Conquest" title="Robert Conquest">Robert Conquest</a> have offered anti-Marxist interpretations of history. <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">Feminist</a> historians such as <a href="/wiki/Joan_Wallach_Scott" title="Joan Wallach Scott">Joan Wallach Scott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claudia_Koonz" title="Claudia Koonz">Claudia Koonz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natalie_Zemon_Davis" title="Natalie Zemon Davis">Natalie Zemon Davis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheila_Rowbotham" title="Sheila Rowbotham">Sheila Rowbotham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gisela_Bock" title="Gisela Bock">Gisela Bock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gerda_Lerner" title="Gerda Lerner">Gerda Lerner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Fox-Genovese" title="Elizabeth Fox-Genovese">Elizabeth Fox-Genovese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Hunt" title="Lynn Hunt">Lynn Hunt</a> have argued for the importance of studying the experience of women in the past. In recent years, <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernists</a> have challenged the validity and need for the study of history on the basis that all history is based on the personal interpretation of sources. In his 1997 book <i>In Defence of History</i>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_J._Evans" title="Richard J. Evans">Richard J. Evans</a>, a professor of modern history at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge University</a>, defended the worth of history. Another defence of history from post-modernist criticism was the Australian historian <a href="/wiki/Keith_Windschuttle" title="Keith Windschuttle">Keith Windschuttle</a>'s 1994 book, <i>The Killing of History</i>.</p>
<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Historians">Historians</span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_historians" title="List of historians">List of historians</a></div>
<p>Professional and amateur historians discover, collect, organize, and present information about past events. In <a href="/wiki/List_of_historians" title="List of historians">lists of historians</a>, historians can be grouped by order of the historical period in which they were writing, which is not necessarily the same as the period in which they specialized. Chroniclers and annalists, though they are not historians in the true sense, are also frequently included.</p>

<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Periods">Periods</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Periodization" title="Periodization">Periodization</a></div>
<p>Historical study often focuses on events and developments that occur in particular blocks of time. Historians give these <a href="/wiki/Periodization" title="Periodization">periods of time</a> names in order to allow "organising ideas and classificatory generalisations" to be used by historians.<sup id="cite_ref-Marwick-p169_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marwick-p169-27"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The names given to a period can vary with geographical location, as can the dates of the start and end of a particular period. <a href="/wiki/Centuries" title="Centuries" class="mw-redirect">Centuries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Decade" title="Decade">decades</a> are commonly used periods and the time they represent depends on the <a href="/wiki/Calendar_era" title="Calendar era">dating system</a> used. Most periods are constructed retrospectively and so reflect value judgments made about the past. The way periods are constructed and the names given to them can affect the way they are viewed and studied.<sup id="cite_ref-Tosh-p168-169_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tosh-p168-169-28"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_locations">Geographical locations</span></h3>
<p>Particular <a href="/wiki/Geographical" title="Geographical" class="mw-redirect">geographical</a> locations can form the basis of historical study, for example, <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Countries" title="Countries" class="mw-redirect">countries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cities" title="Cities" class="mw-redirect">cities</a>. Understanding why historic events took place is important. To do this, historians often turn to <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>. Weather patterns, the water supply, and the landscape of a place all affect the lives of the people who live there. For example, to explain why the ancient Egyptians developed a successful civilization, studying the <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Egypt" title="Geography of Egypt">geography of Egypt</a> is essential. Egyptian civilization was built on the banks of the Nile River, which flooded each year, depositing soil on its banks. The rich soil could help farmers grow enough crops to feed the people in the cities. That meant everyone did not have to farm, so some people could perform other jobs that helped develop the civilization.</p>
<h4> <span class="mw-headline" id="World">World</span></h4>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_world" title="History of the world">History of the world</a></div>
<p>World history is the study of major civilizations over the last 3000 years or so. It has led to highly controversial interpretations by <a href="/wiki/Oswald_Spengler" title="Oswald Spengler">Oswald Spengler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arnold_J._Toynbee" title="Arnold J. Toynbee">Arnold J. Toynbee</a>, among others. World history is especially important as a teaching field. It has increasingly entered the university curriculum in the U.S., in many cases replacing courses in Western Civilization, that had a focus on Europe and the U.S. World history adds extensive new material on Asia, Africa and Latin America.</p>
<h4> <span class="mw-headline" id="Regions">Regions</span></h4>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_Africa" title="History of Africa">History of Africa</a> begins with the first emergence of modern human beings on the continent, continuing into its modern present as a patchwork of diverse and politically developing nation states.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Americas" title="History of the Americas">History of the Americas</a> is the collective history of North and South America, including Central America and the Caribbean.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_North_America" title="History of North America">History of North America</a> is the study of the past passed down from generation to generation on the continent in the Earth's northern and western hemisphere.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_Central_America" title="History of Central America">History of Central America</a> is the study of the past passed down from generation to generation on the continent in the Earth's western hemisphere.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Caribbean" title="History of the Caribbean">History of the Caribbean</a> begins with the oldest evidence where 7,000-year-old remains have been found.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_South_America" title="History of South America">History of South America</a> is the study of the past passed down from generation to generation on the continent in the Earth's southern and western hemisphere.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_Antarctica" title="History of Antarctica">History of Antarctica</a> emerges from early Western theories of a vast continent, known as Terra Australis, believed to exist in the far south of the globe.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_Australia" title="History of Australia">History of Australia</a> start with the documentation of the Makassar trading with Indigenous Australians on Australia's north coast.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_New_Zealand" title="History of New Zealand">History of New Zealand</a> dates back at least 700 years to when it was discovered and settled by Polynesians, who developed a distinct Māori culture centred on kinship links and land.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Pacific_Islands" title="History of the Pacific Islands">History of the Pacific Islands</a> covers the history of the islands in the Pacific Ocean.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_Eurasia" title="History of Eurasia">History of Eurasia</a> is the collective history of several distinct peripheral coastal regions: the Middle East, South Asia, East Asia, Southeast Asia, and Europe, linked by the interior mass of the Eurasian steppe of Central Asia and Eastern Europe.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_Europe" title="History of Europe">History of Europe</a> describes the passage of time from humans inhabiting the European continent to the present day.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_Asia" title="History of Asia">History of Asia</a> can be seen as the collective history of several distinct peripheral coastal regions, East Asia, South Asia, and the Middle East linked by the interior mass of the Eurasian steppe.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_East_Asia" title="History of East Asia">History of East Asia</a> is the study of the past passed down from generation to generation in East Asia.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Middle_East" title="History of the Middle East">History of the Middle East</a> begins with the earliest civilizations in the region now known as the Middle East that were established around 3000 BC, in Mesopotamia (Iraq).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_South_Asia" title="History of South Asia" class="mw-redirect">History of South Asia</a> is the study of the past passed down from generation to generation in the Sub-Himalayan region.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_Southeast_Asia" title="History of Southeast Asia">History of Southeast Asia</a> has been characterized as interaction between regional players and foreign powers.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Military_history">Military history</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Military_history" title="Military history">Military history</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Military_history" title="Military history">Military history</a> concerns warfare, strategies, battles, weapons, and the psychology of combat. The "new military history" since the 1970s has been concerned with soldiers more than generals, with psychology more than tactics, and with the broader impact of warfare on society and culture.<sup id="cite_ref-Pavkovic_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pavkovic-29"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_religion">History of religion</span></h3>
<p>The history of religion has been a main theme for both secular and religious historians for centuries, and continues to be taught in seminaries and academe. Leading journals include Church History, <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Historical_Review" title="Catholic Historical Review" class="mw-redirect">Catholic Historical Review</a>, and <i>History of Religions</i>. Topics range widely from political and cultural and artistic dimensions, to theology and liturgy.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Every major country is covered,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> and most smaller ones as well.</p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Social_history">Social history</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Social_history" title="Social history">Social history</a></div>
<p><i>Social history</i>, sometimes called the <i>new social history,</i> is the field that includes history of ordinary people and their strategies and institutions for coping with life.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> In its "golden age" it was a major growth field in the 1960s and 1970s among scholars, and still is well represented in history departments. In two decades from 1975 to 1995, the proportion of professors of history in American universities identifying with social history rose from 31% to 41%, while the proportion of political historians fell from 40% to 30%.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> In the history departments of British universities in 2007, of the 5723 faculty members, 1644 (29%) identified themselves with <b>social history</b> while <a href="/wiki/Political_history" title="Political history">political history</a> came next with 1425 (25%).<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> The "old" social history before the 1960s was a hodgepodge of topics without a central theme, and it often included political movements, like Populism, that were "social" in the sense of being outside the elite system. Social history was contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Political_history" title="Political history">political history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_history" title="Intellectual history">intellectual history</a> and the history of <a href="/wiki/Great_man_theory" title="Great man theory" class="mw-redirect">great men</a>. English historian <a href="/wiki/G._M._Trevelyan" title="G. M. Trevelyan">G. M. Trevelyan</a> saw it as the bridging point between economic and political history, reflecting that, "Without social history, economic history is barren and political history unintelligible."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> While the field has often been viewed negatively as history with the politics left out, it has also been defended as "history with the people put back in."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_history">Cultural history</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cultural_history" title="Cultural history">Cultural history</a></div>
<p>Cultural history replaced <a href="/wiki/Social_history" title="Social history">social history</a> as the dominant form in the 1980s and 1990s. It typically combines the approaches of anthropology and history to look at language, popular cultural traditions and cultural interpretations of historical experience. It examines the records and narrative descriptions of past knowledge, customs, and arts of a group of people. How peoples constructed their memory of the past is a major topic.</p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Diplomatic_history">Diplomatic history</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_history" title="Diplomatic history">Diplomatic history</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_history" title="Diplomatic history">Diplomatic history</a>, sometimes referred to as "<i>Rankian History</i>"<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> in honor of <a href="/wiki/Leopold_von_Ranke" title="Leopold von Ranke">Leopold von Ranke</a>, focuses on <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>, politicians and other high rulers and views them as being the driving force of continuity and change in history. This type of <i>political history</i> is the study of the conduct of <a href="/wiki/International_relations#History" title="International relations">international relations</a> between states or across state boundaries over time. This is the most common form of history and is often the classical and popular belief of what history should be.</p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_history">Economic history</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economic_history" title="Economic history">Economic history</a></div>
<p>Although economic history has been well established since the late 19th century, in recent years academic studies have shifted more and more toward economics departments and away from traditional history departments.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_history">Environmental history</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_history" title="Environmental history">Environmental history</a></div>
<p>Environmental history is a new field that emerged in the 1980s to look at the history of the environment, especially in the long run, and the impact of human activities upon it.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="World_history">World history</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/World_History" title="World History">World History</a></div>
<p>World history is primarily a teaching field, rather than a research field. It gained popularity in the United States,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Japan<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> and other countries after the 1980s with the realization that students need a broader exposure to the world as globalization proceeds.</p>
<p>The World History Association publishes the <i>Journal of World History</i> every quarter since 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> The H-World discussion list<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> serves as a network of communication among practitioners of world history, with discussions among scholars, announcements, syllabi, bibliographies and book reviews.</p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="People.27s_history">People's history</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/People%27s_history" title="People's history">People's history</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/People%27s_history" title="People's history">people's history</a> is a type of historical work which attempts to account for historical events from the <a href="/wiki/Populism" title="Populism">perspective of common people</a>. A people's history is the history of the world that is the story of mass movements and of the outsiders. Individuals or groups not included in the past in other type of writing about history are the primary focus, which includes the <a href="/wiki/Disfranchisement" title="Disfranchisement">disenfranchised</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Oppression" title="Oppression">oppressed</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poor</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nonconformism" title="Nonconformism">nonconformists</a>, and the otherwise forgotten people. This history also usually focuses on events occurring in the fullness of time, or when an overwhelming wave of smaller events cause certain developments to occur.</p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Historiometry">Historiometry</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Historiometry" title="Historiometry">Historiometry</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Historiometry" title="Historiometry">Historiometry</a> is a historical study of human progress or individual personal characteristics, by using statistics to analyze references to eminent persons, their statements, behavior and discoveries in relatively neutral texts.</p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_history">Gender history</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gender_history" title="Gender history">Gender history</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gender_history" title="Gender history">Gender history</a> is a sub-field of History and <a href="/wiki/Gender_studies" title="Gender studies">Gender studies</a>, which looks at the past from the perspective of <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a>. It is in many ways, an outgrowth of <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_history" title="Women's history">women's history</a>. Despite its relatively short life, Gender History (and its forerunner Women's History) has had a rather significant effect on the general study of history. Since the 1960s, when the initially small field first achieved a measure of acceptance, it has gone through a number of different phases, each with its own challenges and outcomes. Although some of the changes to the study of history have been quite obvious, such as increased numbers of books on famous women or simply the admission of greater numbers of women into the historical profession, other influences are more subtle.</p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Public_history">Public history</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Public_history" title="Public history">Public history</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Public_history" title="Public history">Public history</a> is a term that describes the broad range of activities undertaken by people with some training in the discipline of history who are generally working outside of specialized academic settings. Public history practice has quite deep roots in the areas of historic preservation, archival science, oral history, museum curatorship, and other related fields. The term itself began to be used in the U.S. and Canada in the late 1970s, and the field has become increasingly professionalized since that time. Some of the most common settings for public history are museums, historic homes and historic sites, parks, battlefields, archives, film and television companies, and all levels of government.</p>
<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudohistory">Pseudohistory</span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pseudohistory" title="Pseudohistory">Pseudohistory</a></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Pseudohistory" title="Pseudohistory">Pseudohistory</a></i> is a term applied to texts which purport to be historical in nature but which depart from standard <a href="/wiki/Historical_method" title="Historical method">historiographical conventions</a> in a way which undermines their conclusions. Closely related to deceptive <a href="/wiki/Historical_revisionism_(negationism)" title="Historical revisionism (negationism)">historical revisionsm</a>, works which draw controversial conclusions from new, speculative, or disputed historical evidence, particularly in the fields of national, political, military, and religious affairs, are often rejected as pseudohistory.</p>
<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Teaching_history">Teaching history</span></h2>
<p>From the origins of national school systems in the 19th century, the teaching of history to promote national sentiment has been a high priority. In the United States after World War I, a strong movement emerged at the university level to teach courses in Western Civilization, so as to give students a common heritage with Europe. In the U.S. after 1980 attention increasingly moved toward teaching <a href="/wiki/World_history" title="World history" class="mw-redirect">world history</a> or requiring students to take courses in non-western cultures, to prepare students for life in a globalized economy.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At the university level, historians debate the question of whether history belongs more to social science or to the humanities. Many view the field from both perspectives.</p>
<p>The teaching of history in French schools was influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Nouvelle_histoire" title="Nouvelle histoire">Nouvelle histoire</a> as disseminated after the 1960s by <i>Cahiers pédagogiques and Enseignement</i> and other journals for teachers. Also influential was the Institut national de recherche et de documentation pédagogique, (INRDP). Joseph Leif, the Inspector-general of teacher training, said pupils children should learn about historians’ approaches as well as facts and dates. Louis François, Dean of the History/Geography group in the Inspectorate of National Education advised that teachers should provide historic documents and promote "active methods" which would give pupils "the immense happiness of discovery." Proponents said it was a reaction against the memorization of names and dates that characterized teaching and left the students bored. Traditionalists protested loudly it was a postmodern innovation that threatened to leave the youth ignorant of French patriotism and national identity.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3> <span class="mw-headline" id="Political_correctness">Political correctness</span></h3>
<div class="rellink boilerplate seealso">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Political_historical_revisionism" title="Political historical revisionism" class="mw-redirect">political historical revisionism</a>.</div>
<p>In most countries history textbook are tools to foster nationalism and patriotism, and give students the official line about national enemies.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In many countries history textbooks are sponsored by the national government and are written to put the national heritage in the most favorable light. For example, in Japan, mention of the <a href="/wiki/Nanking_Massacre" title="Nanking Massacre">Nanking Massacre</a> has been removed from textbooks and the entire World War II is given cursory treatment. Other countries have complained.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> It was standard policy in communist countries to present only a rigid Marxist historiography.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States the history of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> was phrased to avoid giving offense to white Southerners and blacks.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Academic historians have often fought against the politicization of the textbooks, sometimes with success.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 21st-century Germany, the history curriculum is controlled by the 16 states, and is characterized not by superpatriotism but rather by an "almost pacifistic and deliberately unpatriotic undertone" and reflects "principles formulated by international organizations such as UNESCO or the Council of Europe, thus oriented towards human rights, democracy and peace." The result is that "German textbooks usually downplay national pride and ambitions and aim to develop an understanding of citizenship centred on democracy, progress, human rights, peace, tolerance and Europeanness."<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
